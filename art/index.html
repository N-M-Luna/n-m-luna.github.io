Here is a little bit about me.

I was born and raised in Puerto Rico. I moved to Texas to get my master's in math. Since the pandemic, I've been living in Sacramento, CA and learning how to use watercolors. I find them extremely challenging but equally satisfying.

Sprouting Boot © 2023 is licensed under CC-BY-NC-SA 4.0.

More often than not, my watercolors end up with some ink outlines. Some strong influences are Traditional American Tattoos and punk and rock music. (Fun fact: Teenage Luna was obssessed with the album jacket for Americana by The Offspring.)

Ruin © 2024 is licensed under CC-BY-NC-SA 4.0.

Science fiction in any form is an instant favorite of mine. Some of my favorite writers are Orson Scott Card, H. G. Wells, Ray Bradbury, and pretty much anyone writing about Star Trek. I love mid-century science fiction where space exploration was in vogue and vaguely humanoid household bots did our chores.

Inkbot © 2023 is licensed under CC-BY-NC-SA 4.0.

I started ballet young and was able to enjoy it for about 10 years. I've also danced jazz, salsa, belly dance, modern/contemporary, spanish folk, hip-hop, and pole dance. It's always a pleasure to sketch, draw and paint the shapes that dancers do with their bodies, costumes, and props.

The Roaring Dancer © 2023 is licensed under CC-BY-NC-SA 4.0.

Just having the support to create new interesting illustrations and personal paintings is enough for me. But I would be thrilled to be given the chance to illustrate a book. =)

Just do it! Start that journal! Paint that canvas!

1 dec 2025.

Brief reflection on an artist's irrational desire to wait to be "good enough" to do use expensive materials or to sumbit their work. And comments on overcoming it.

I'm sure I'm not the only person to have entered a state of paralysis and mild panic when encoutnered with an empty canvas. And also the frustration that comes from seeing that the paint I did manage to splatter on it is not even close to resembling the masterpiece I sketched in my imagination. This has gone on long enough so that I have learned to "save" my good paints and the nice papers to whenever I deem myself "good enough".

There is just one problem with this logic. I can never hope to improve at all if I never practice. While my conscious mind recognized this, my eyes did not want to accept that this necessary practice often looked like not great art.

There is this very interesting study in this book. (quote and reference betty's book)

In the end, the not great art that I suffered myself to make turned out to be not so bad art. And sometimes my "painting exercises" turn out to be depictions or pictures I personally like. No, not always; sometimes I finish a painting and know it's not one of my best works. But I feel satisfied that I tried and now know what does not work.

My 1000-step process for painting watercolors

1 mar 2026.

An outline of my watercolor paintng process. It starts with sketches, followed by a final ink outline drawing. Then I do a color study, where I pick color hues and families. From this, I make the pencil outline on the actual watercolor paper. Then (and only then!) the actual painting begins; lightest part to darkest part of the image.
